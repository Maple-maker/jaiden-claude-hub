Section 01

The lazy-operator move

Most of the work that drains your week is repeatable: the morning inbox triage, the Friday content draft, the weekly status roundup. None of it needs you sitting there live. You can describe it once and let a scheduled task carry it from then on.

Leverage gets mythologized as doing more. It's the opposite — it's repeating less. The professionally lazy operator builds the machine once and stops re-doing the job.

The reframeDon't ask 'how do I do this faster?' Ask 'how do I never have to do this again?'
Section 02

Speak it in plain English

You don't need cron syntax or a scheduler UI. You say when in normal words — 'every weekday at 6am', 'each Friday afternoon', 'on the 1st of the month' — and what you want done. Claude turns that into a recurring task.

If you can describe the chore to a person, you can schedule it.

Every weekday at 6:00am:
summarize my unread email into one short brief,
flag anything that needs a reply today.
Section 03

It runs without you

At the time you set, the task fires and Claude does the work autonomously — no prompt from you, no babysitting. It gathers what it needs, performs the steps, and delivers the output where you asked for it.

The point is the asymmetry: a few minutes of setup buys you a result that arrives every day, indefinitely.

Key ideaA scheduled task converts a recurring chore into a one-time decision.
Section 04

Review, don't operate

Your job changes from operator to overseer. Instead of running the steps, you read the result and decide if it's right. Over a week or two you tighten the instructions until the output is exactly what you'd have produced by hand.

Build the machine once, then tend it. A single well-tuned task can erase an entire weekly chore from your life.

In your sandboxPick one chore you do every week. Schedule it. Spend the next two weeks tuning the prompt, not doing the chore.
